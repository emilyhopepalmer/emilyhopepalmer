About

I am a visiting professor of Physics at the Cooper Union and a visiting researcher in Biological & Environmental Engineering at Cornell University. I've always been fascinated by the interplay between engineered and biological systems, which, while sometimes treated as opposites, can form a union enabling not only better understanding of both, but also novel work that could never have been produced through study of one discipline alone. My research draws on expertise in fluid dynamics, biomechanics, and neuroscience to develop a deep understanding of the neural control and dynamics of biological systems, inform a new class of bioinspired autonomous systems, and provide insight into how the natural world has evolved robust systems of flow control.

Projects

Sensorimotor basis of flight control in Drosophila melanogaster

EH Palmer, JJ Omoto, & MH Dickinson. The role of a population of descending neurons in the optomotor response in flying Drosophila. bioRxiv (2022). https://doi.org/10.1101/2022.12.05.519224.

To maintain controlled flight, animals and aerial vehicles alike must execute continuous trimming adjustments to compensate for internal and external perturbations. Prior work has suggested that, much like engineered systems, flying animals are able to synthesize visual, vestibular, and proprioceptive cues via a PID controller to maintain stability along a desired flight path. I am investigating the neuronal structures underlying visually-mediated flight stabilization to provide new insights into how insects compute the operations they need for controlled flight and suggest new design concepts for autonomous flying vehicles.

Tandem wing interactions in gliding dragonflies

In progress.

Dragonflies have long fascinated scientists as some of the most acrobatic fliers in nature; they expertly capture prey insects during flight, manuever in cluttered environments with ease, and effortlessly transition between flapping and gliding. Their unique tandem wing physiology is of particular interest to aerospace engineers, given the interactions between the wake of the leading wing and the dynamics of the trailing wing, but much of the research on these interactions has focused on active flapping as opposed to gliding. In this project, I use a dynamically-scaled model of a pair of dragonfly wings to show that in gliding flight, vortices shed from the first wing inject momentum into the boundary layer of the second, keeping the boundary layer attached at high angles of attack and increasing the lift-to-drag ratio.

Computational modeling of search behavior

AH Behbahani*, EH Palmer*, RA Corfas, & MH Dickinson. Drosophila re-zero their path integrator at the center of a fictive food patch. Current Biology (2021). https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cub.2021.08.006.

Many animals rely on the ability to return to a key location after venturing away from that site, famously including the ability of honeybees to return to the hive after finding a food source and communicating its location to conspecifics. Similarly, fruit flies, upon finding a patch of food, will perform local searches in the vicinity of the original patch to order to locate more desirable food sources. In this project, I developed a set of state-transition models to describe the algorithmic basis of idiothetic path integration in fruit flies. Through experiments testing predictions made by the various hypotheses, we were able to determine that, when multiple food sources are present, the flies center their searches at the centroid of the patch, rather than at a particular food site.

Aeromechanics of the long jump in Rhaphidophoridae

EH Palmer, N Deshler, R Mittal. Aeromechanics Of Long Jumps In Spider Crickets: Insights From Experiments And Modeling. ASME IMECE 2017, Tampa, Florida, November 3-9, 2017, https://doi.org/10.1115/IMECE2017-73498.

Spider crickets are among the most proficient long-jumpers in nature, capable of jumping more than 60 times their body length without tumbling through the use of their legs as control surfaces while aloft. I combined high-speed videogrammetry experiments with aerodynamic modelling to demonstrate that the crickets assume a posture during their descent that is stable to pitch perturbations. Jumping is a unique form of locomotion, straddling the terrestrial and the aerial; insights derived from this study not only provide biomechanical insight but may also serve as inspiration for small jumping robots.

Teaching

History, Ethics, and Identity in STEM

EH Palmer, JR Tawney, J Weaver. A Model for Student-led Development and Implementation of a Required Graduate-level Course on History, Ethics, and Identity in Aerospace Engineering. ASEE Annual Conference & Exposition Proceedings, Minneapolis, Minnesota, June 2022.

In 2020, I proposed, developed, and implemented an update to a required seminar series for first year graduate students in aerospace engineering to include lectures and discussions on history, ethics, and identity in STEM, with invited speakers giving talks on topics ranging from the history of Einstein’s development of relativity through the Cold War, to ethical arguments on the Boeing 737 MAX disasters, to inclusive strategies for teaching mathematics. Following a successful pilot program in the aerospace engineering department in AY2020-2021, I expanded the series in AY2021-2022 to advertise to and host discussion groups for students across the university, with funding from the Engineering and Applied Sciences New Horizons DEI Fund. Through my program, the department has recognized the critical importance of engaging with the human context within which we practice engineering and claimed ownership over ensuring that ours is a culture in which it is prioritized.

Aerospace Engineering Qualifying Exam Bootcamp

Graduate students in aerospace engineering must satisfactorily perform during a series of three, hour-long oral exams in mathematics, and their choice of two of solids, fluids, and controls if they wish to proceed to PhD study. Despite the importance of these exams, there has historically been little support for students as they prepare; in particular, there are no opportunities to formally practice working on problems at the board while describing their thought processes out loud. To provide a mechanism to develop this skill, I developed a preparatory bootcamp, consisting of a series of progressive practice sessions during which students first became comfortable at the board with nontechnical material or material that they have otherwise mastered before progressing to working through novel or difficult technical problems. In addition to developing boardwork skills, students also had the opportunity to meet with occupational therapy specialists to ensure healthy work habits during the long and sometimes isolating study period.
